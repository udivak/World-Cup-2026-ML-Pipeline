This report explains, end to end, what has been built, how the prediction models are trained and work, how they perform, and what to do next. The core idea: rate a national team by the players actually in its squad (FIFA/EA-FC attributes) rather than by the team's name or history — because the World Cup churns squads every four years, so a rating attached to "Brazil" assumes a continuity that roster turnover breaks.

02 — How the models are trained & work

From a squad list to a calibrated W/D/L probability

Step 1 — Turn each squad into a profile

For a tournament edition, every player on the 26-man roster is matched to their nearest-prior FIFA snapshot (the most recent edition released before the tournament). Players are mapped to four units (GK/DEF/MID/ATT), the best XI fills the formation, and the rest are depth. The squad is aggregated into one team_profiles row: positional unit strengths, star power, depth, mean age, market value, top-5-league share, mentality (composure), and — added in the enrichment — international reputation, potential, and an elite-player count.

Step 2 — Turn each match into features (the single train/serve code path)

A match becomes one feature row: for every profile column, the difference between the two teams (team1 − team2, team1 = home), plus context (home_adv = 0 at neutral venues, cross_conf). All 18 differences are stored; the models consume a parsimonious 9-feature subset ("ingest wide, select narrow"): diff_overall_xi diff_mean_potential diff_mean_intl_rep diff_elite_count diff_star_power diff_depth diff_mean_composure home_adv cross_conf . The match result (H/D/A) is the label — never a feature.

No leakage — the central discipline

The squad/profile is a legitimate pre-match input (squads are announced before a tournament), but every player attribute feeding it must be dated before the match. A June-2018 World Cup match uses FIFA 18 (released Sep 2017), never FIFA 19 (Sep 2018). This is enforced by tests and the across-edition split.

Step 3 — The candidates

Two reference baselines set the bar, and two profile models (plus their ensemble) try to beat it. All five output calibrated 3-way probabilities through the same machinery:

  • Elo-only baseline — a chronological football-Elo rolled over the entire 49,400-match history (each match's pre-match rating difference), mapped to W/D/L by a logit. The classic team-identity bar. Elo is only a baseline — never a feature in the profile models.
  • Squad-overall baseline — a logit on the single diff_overall_xi (+ home advantage). The naive bottom-up bar.
  • LogReg profile model — median-impute → standardize → regularized multinomial logistic regression. Interpretable, small-N friendly; gives signed coefficients.
  • HGB profile model — HistGradientBoosting trees; handles missing values natively and captures non-linear interactions.
  • Ensemble — the average of the two profile models' probabilities.

Step 4 — Training & evaluation: across-edition expanding window

Models are never evaluated on a random split (that would let a 2024 match train on 2024 data). Instead the cutoff rolls forward one tournament at a time: train on every edition that finished before edition k, predict edition k, advance. Predictions are pooled across all 14 qualifying editions (583 held-out matches, requiring ≥100 prior training matches). Every model is probability-calibrated (Platt/sigmoid, fit on training folds only) so a stated "60% win" really happens ~60% of the time.

Step 5 — How performance is scored

The primary metric is RPS (Ranked Probability Score). Unlike accuracy or log-loss it respects the ordering of outcomes (win → draw → loss): predicting a draw when the team won is a smaller error than predicting a loss. Lower is better; bookmaker-grade ≈ 0.19.

Worked example. Outcomes ordered [win, draw, loss]; prediction [0.60, 0.30, 0.10], actual = win. Compare cumulative distributions: predicted [0.60, 0.90] vs actual [1, 1] → RPS = ½·[(0.60−1)² + (0.90−1)²] = 0.085 (good). The same prediction when the actual is a loss scores 0.385 — RPS punishes being confidently wrong on the far outcome. We also report log-loss (probability sharpness), accuracy, and per-class precision/recall.

Product model — a blend of Elo + profile not a gate pass

The gate above forbids Elo as a model feature, so the pure bottom-up model is the honest research result. For the live 2026 product we may still serve a blend of the two models' outputs — P = w·profile + (1−w)·Elo — a product decision that can never count as a bottom-up gate pass. We pool the already-calibrated, leakage-free fold predictions (no new features, no retraining); the weight is chosen nested (on strictly-prior editions only) so the reported number is honest.

Nested blend (served, linear, w=0.20): RPS 0.1815 vs Elo 0.1802 ✗  ·  log-loss 0.9555 vs Elo 0.9489 ✗  →  does NOT beat Elo

04 — Next steps

Why it falls short, and the levers to close the gap

  • The remaining gap is recent form. Elo absorbs results up to kickoff and decades of accumulated team quality — information the bottom-up design deliberately excludes (match history is a label, never a feature). A squad's FIFA snapshot is up to ~8 months stale and says nothing about current momentum.
  • Confederation coverage is uneven. Gold Cup / Asian Cup / AFCON squads match only ~11–16 of 26 players to FIFA, making those profiles noisy — exactly the editions where Elo wins biggest.
  • The enrichment doesn't yet reach 2026. potential and international_reputation are absent from the loaded FC26 dataset, so the gains help the backtest but not (yet) live WC2026 scoring.

Concretely, the immediate engineering options:

Re-ingest FC26 with full attributes
Unlocks the enrichment for live 2026 scoring and enables more features. Reuses the FIFA loader.
Proceed to Phase 4
Assemble 48 squads, score 104 fixtures, Monte-Carlo the bracket with the current model.
Ingest Football Manager
Independent signal; bigger lift (empty data dir, second canonicalization, correlated with FIFA).
Relax the no-form rule
Allow a form/Elo signal — likely beats the baseline, but changes the research question.

05 — Suggestions

Recommended path, prioritized

My professional read: the bottom-up thesis is partly validated — squad talent carries real, well-calibrated signal and beats Elo on rich-coverage editions — but pure squad attributes are unlikely to fully overtake Elo, whose edge is recent form. We tested the obvious shortcut — blending the profile model with Elo — and it does not beat Elo out-of-sample (the errors are too correlated, 0.774). The honest split therefore stands: keep the pure model as the research artifact (the gate), serve a modest blend as the 2026 product, and invest in the one lever that can actually win — a recent-form signal. Ordered by value-for-effort:

The way to actually beat Elo is to add the ingredient it has and the profile lacks — recent form / momentum / squad cohesion — as a real feature.
